Essay
  • library_booksEssential Question

    Why should high schoolers learn computer programming?

  • library_booksIntroduction

    Throughout the progression of the industrialized United States, literacy has been the foundation for economic expansion and improved standards of living. During the Golden Age following WWII, Americans who came from basic backgrounds in reading, writing and mathematics, were able to make a comfortable living after graduating high school. Since then, the middle-class has folded, forcing high school graduates to pursue expensive, higher forms of education to appease the growing demand of a more qualified workforce. The recent development of computer technology has since created a vacuum for professionals to fill because there isn’t the sufficient workforce to back the demand. Considering the wealth generated by computer innovations and our desire to continue dominating the world economically, high school literacy must expand in the 21st century beyond traditional reading and writing and include proficiency in computer programming.

  • library_booksFirst Body

    The workers of the automotive, technology, and aviation industries that were key contributors towards our economic expansion after WWII generally came from unions (U.S. Department of State). High school level literacy was the foundation of that labor pool since many of them had the most basic level of education required, a high school diploma and or trade skills. Avenues for higher levels of education like college or graduate school were available and relatively cheap, but the requirements for such degrees weren’t necessary for middle-class occupations. After the end of WWII, the American population rapidly increased. A bigger population created a wider job pool for employers to select from. But coupled with a declining economy in the 70s, employers were forced to be more selective about who they hired if they wanted to remain a profitable business. The solution for determining who was more qualified was simply done by looking for college graduates.

  • library_booksSecond Body

    Since the baby boom, there has been more college graduates than previously before in American history. But college isn’t cheap and school generally isn’t a thing an average kid desires to pursue. The reason for, “enrollments in high schools and in colleges or universities,” to increase, ”respectively, from 57.7 and 10.3 percent of the eligible students in 1960 to 91.9 and 37.8 percent in 1975,” is because of the increasing demand of higher education from well-paying employers (Britannica). The increased amount of enrollments directly made universities hire more staff, subsequently making college more expensive and less available to those without the money to enroll. Today, there is not enough Computer Science graduates (most common degree for becoming a software developer) to meet the industry’s demand. I propose that high school students begin learning how to write computer code because of the well-paying opportunities available for software developers and the availability of free resources on the Internet.

  • library_booksThird Body

    It is comfortable to assert that literacy requirements and educational standards in K-12 must expand beyond their previous benchmarks to teach kids how to read and write code. America will fall behind in the global economic competition if it doesn’t fill the vacuum of software developers. In 2016, technology companies made up, “7.1 percent of overall GDP and 11.6 percent of total private sector payroll,” (Ostrowski). Tech companies generally require college degrees, and most of the employees are software engineers. Computer science, specifically coding skills, are a core part of the educational foundation and practical skills needed in tech companies. Math and language skills are also mandatory, but they are still not sufficient enough. Coding proficiency is the new, minimum level required for employment at tech companies that offer, “free food, unlimited paid vacation, travel stipends — and don't forget super cool offices,” (McGee).

  • library_booksConclusion

    Today, the educational requirements to be a middle-class citizen are expensive and highly competitive to be considered as a possible student. Literacy of reading and writing did provide meaningful jobs to workers during the post-war era, but employers have since evolved to being more selective. High schoolers should learn how to code to open possible well-paying job opportunities. Even if coding isn’t a desirable profession, it does provide insight towards computers, devices which are in use in almost all professions nowadays.
